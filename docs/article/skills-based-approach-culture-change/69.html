The skills-based approach to culture change

Article
by bryceb

ReLeaf and the skills-based approach to culture change as explained by Per Hugander both strive to achieve lasting change, though they operate in distinct spaces: ReLeaf in the realm of urban greening, and the skills-based approach in the organizational context. 

Hugander's model emphasizes infusing new culture components into the organization through a skills-based approach, a method particularly effective for developing soft skills such as perspective taking. This approach parallels ReLeaf's model of transforming urban spaces into thriving ecosystems: both demand a new perspective and the capacity to visualize alternative futures, whether in organizational culture or urban landscapes.

ReLeaf, much like the skills-based approach, aims to link immediate tangible outcomes with long-term goals. Just as organizations tie new skills to bottom-line results, ReLeaf associates the transformation of urban spaces with immediate and long-term ecological, social, and economic benefits. 

However, the divergence lies in the subject of transformation. Hugander focuses on organizational culture and the individuals within it, arguing for change by altering underlying assumptions about success. On the other hand, ReLeaf targets physical spaces, though it also influences the cultural perspective towards urban environments, advocating for a shift from seeing these spaces as static to recognizing their potential as dynamic, green ecosystems.

Another crucial distinction is the medium of change. Hugander's model operates through the vehicle of employees within an organization, using training and development programs to foster new skills. ReLeaf, instead, uses the environment as its primary medium of change, albeit still requiring human participation and effort to bring these transformations to life.

In conclusion, both models stress the importance of active participation, perspective shifts, and tangible outcomes in enacting meaningful change. They do, however, apply these principles to different ends: one to organizational culture, the other to urban greening.
