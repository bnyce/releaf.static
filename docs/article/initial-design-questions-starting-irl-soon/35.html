Initial design questions, starting up IRL soon

Article
by bryceb

Thanks, trashrobot, for asking, and the short answer is: we need to start building these immediately and continue to adapt them and adjust them as needed and as we learn. For each business' waste and each project, it takes some creativity to find the simplest way to modularize the pieces into useful components and/or how to integrate and attach modules to form bigger compositions. The commercially available system, LiveWall, is basically a set of shelves. That makes the planter grid easy, but I think there's room for multiple sizes of containers that still cover the entire sun-facing side resulting in far more interesting and sustainable ecosystems.

We're looking for the right balance between variety, customization and modularity. So, it depends on the source as well as the destination, but we are always seeking patterns, recipes, batches, and easy-repurposing.

Here's a start on some answers, but of course it will be so much better when we demonstrate these details and more in real life (soon!)

  • Plant species: Choosing a plant species for each module would depend on various factors, including the micro climate of its eventual home, the height of the garden wall and its position, maintenance needs, and aesthetic preferences. A mix of hardy, evergreen plants would likely be a good choice. Creeping or trailing plants could provide good coverage for a sound wall. Examples might include ivy, climbing hydrangea, or jasmine. 
  • Planter design: The design for the planters would need to be functional, aesthetic, and durable. They would need to provide sufficient space for the roots, retain enough moisture for the plant's needs, and withstand outdoor conditions. The design might also include ways to attach or secure the planters to the garden wall structure. 
  • Material for planters: Options for planter materials include wood, metal, plastic, or other upcycled materials. Each material has its pros and cons. Wood is natural and durable but may deteriorate over time. Metal is very durable, but may not be the best for plant health if it gets too hot or cold. Plastic is lightweight and versatile, but may not be as sustainable or attractive. Whatever is ugly or mishaped needs to be beneath some sort of cover layer.
  • Size of planters: The size of each planter would depend on the size and growth habits of the selected plants, as well as the design of the garden wall. For a trailing plant like ivy, smaller planters spaced relatively closely together might be effective. For larger shrubs or trees, larger planters would be needed. 
  • Existing research: There is some research suggesting that green walls or "living walls" can provide sound insulation benefits. The exact effectiveness can depend on many factors, including the density and type of plants, and the construction of the wall or support structure. Researching these studies and existing designs would be a good starting point for this project.
